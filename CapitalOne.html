Capital One

Software Engineering Internship
June 2019-August 2019
Technologies Used: AWS (EMR, S3, and Lambda), PySpark, Jenkins

In the event of a third party data breach, Capital One has the obligation to identify which of its customers were potentially impacted and to notify them accordingly. When the Equifax breach happened in 2017 (over 150 million people's information compromised), Equifax sent Capital One all the customer data that had been potentially affected so that Capital One could identify all impacted customers.
However, the manner in which Capital One went about identifying its customers from the Equifax breach was extremely unsafe. The social security numbers of all the breached Equifax customers as well as all Capital One customers were sitting in raw plaintext form on some on-premise server, where data analysts would run SQL queries to match breached Equifax accounts to Capital One customers based on social security numbers.
Now that Capital One has gone all in on the cloud (all of its infrastructure is now hosted on AWS), that method of customer identification would be unacceptable for various security reasons such as having plaintext Social Security Numbers sitting at rest, giving human access to all this sensitive information, etc.
My internship project was to develop an automated system to do all the customer matching so that a human would not have access to such a large amount of sensitive information. To mitigate having plaintext Social security Numbers sitting at rest, Capital One has now implemented tokeniziation on all sensitive data within the organization. When a third party sends Capital One all breached accounts, all sensitive information in those accounts will also be tokenized at the frontier. So instead of matching on plaintext Social Security Numbers, my system would match on their tokenized counterparts. Given the characteristics of tokenization, a match on tokens means a match on social security numbers...simple enough, right?
I built out a contained environment to execute all the matching safely. My match engine uses a highly restricted AWS S3 bucket and a Lambda (to be automatically provisioned by my code when Capital One gets notified of a third party data breach). The Lambda expects the information all of third party breached individuals to land in the S3 bucket (in a CSV format) and will deploy the matching code (which was written in PySpark) onto a provisioned Elastic MapReduce cluster, pull in all Capital One accounts from its data lake, and identify all customers. Then it will write all the results back into the same S3 bucket that was originally provisioned.
